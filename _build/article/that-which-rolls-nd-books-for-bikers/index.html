That Which Rolls - ND Books for Bikers

That Which Rolls - ND Books for Bikers

The other day a little girl was learning how to ride a bike in the park near my house. Though she was doing well where the pavement was smooth, where it buckled, she often got stuck. Her training wheels having hoisted the back wheel off the ground, she pedaled fruitlessly and, once, fell down. How unlike this girl was from the one evoked in Forrest Gander’s “Lovegreen” from Core Samples from the World, “the girl open-mouthed / pumping her two-wheeler / across a meadow softly-furred / as a bumble bee, her plastic bag / pendent with hard apples / from one handlebar swaying.” The critical difference, of course: Gander’s girl has no training wheels.


Photo from Core Samples from the World
Photo by Raymond Meeks

This summer, if you’re biking without a good book in your backpack, you’re incurring a similar misfortune. Biking bookless is the practiced cyclist’s training wheels; you might be whizzing about corporally, but you’re in spiritual stasis. Fortunately, the bookshelves at New Directions are packed with remedies, reads to keep your back wheel rolling all summer long. Without further ado: books on bikes.

First up is a short story by Guy Davenport entitled “The Richard Nixon Freishütz Rag.” In a spectacularly knotted nine pages, Richard Nixon tours China, and an amorous couple swoons in a summer field. But it’s the middle passage that’s the real gear crank of the tale. Here, Davenport transports us to Renaissance Italy, where Leonardo Da Vinci is about to complete his new invention: the “two-wheeled balancing machine.” Though Leonardo is at first enthusiastic about his creation, picturing boys soaring “like the wind, like Ezekiel’s angel, like the horses of Ancona,” he soon lapses into melancholy as he considers the final component. What chance is there for progress and liberty when even the bicycle—at last releasing the human form from the terrible burden of being two-legged and upright—requires a chain?

While Leonardo sees a limitation in the fact that a bike needs a chain, Raymond Queneau, co-founder of Oulipo—a collective of authors and mathematicians dedicated to exploring constrained writing—might more likely find potential. Like a bicycle, many of Queneau’s works also require chains—chains of his own devising—constraints  capable of delivering a person (writer/reader/rider) somewhere new and unexpected. It’s fitting that his final novel, The Flight of Icarus features a protagonist obsessed with mechanical locomotion. Icarus is a character that has escaped from the first pages of a poorly developed manuscript. (“How extremely Pirandellian,” one character comments.) Though he seems to enjoy what absinthe and sex fin-de-siècle Paris has to offer, Icarus finds complete satisfaction only in the pursuit of speed, a penchant he discovers during his first bike ride:
    I feel I’m becoming a poet
    Astride my bike—watch me go it!


Philippe Noiret (left) as Pablo Neruda in Il Postino

Queneau’s Icarus isn’t the only poet to sing the praises of the most popular vehicle in the world. Pablo Neruda takes the same muse for a spin in his “Ode to Bicycles.” Describing passing cyclists as “the only / insects / in / that dry / moment of summer / silent / swift / translucent / they / barely stirred / the air,” Neruda evokes the envy all pedestrians must feel as we race gracefully past them. Later, finding a bicycle at rest, the poet ponders its lifelessness: “only moving / does it have a soul.” And how like our bicycles we cyclists are: only with sufficient soul (supplied by good books) can we actually move.

But however awful it must be to be a cyclist prevented from cycling, it must be much worse to be a storage shed. Such is the unenviable fate of the protagonist of Victor Pelevin’s story “The Life and Adventures of Shed Number XII” from his collection 4 By Pelevin. Here, the eponymous structure eludes his dread of becoming a pickling shed by dreaming he is one of the bicycles stored inside him. When his worst nightmare becomes a reality and the bikes are replaced by casks of sauerkraut, our hero loses all sense of self. Only the incidental approach of a passing cyclist, ringing his bell, restores the shed to his true identity: “It was enough—Number XII remembered: A bicycle. A highway. A sunset. A bridge over a river.”


Henry Miller

If the self is only discovered in relation to a bicycle, it only makes sense that Henry Miller, that ardent admirer of self, highly prized his ride. In “Paris and Its Suburbs” from The Henry Miller Reader, Miller reflects on his time in France, mostly by reveling in his memories of cycling through it: “...this still mirror bearing the clouds moving transversally I, a complete corporate entity, a universe bringing countless centuries to a conclusion, I and this that passes beneath me and this that floats above me and all that surges through me, I and this, I and that joined up in one continuous movement, this Seine and every Seine that is spanned by a bridge is the miracle of a man crossing it on a bicycle.” Who could help but be swept along in Miller’s grandiose, track-bike lyricism?


Alfred Jarry

But it’s Alfred Jarry, author of the infamous Ubu plays and inventor of ‘pataphysics, who most effectively weds the holy spirit of the reader with the body of the rider. Jarry so adored his velo that he christened it ce qui roule—that which rolls—and wore his cycling outfit at all times. His nearly religious admiration finds no more perfect expression than the short story “The Crucifiction Considered as an Uphill Bike Race.” As the title suggests, Jarry’s retelling revises the Scriptures, insisting that Christ wasn’t actually crucified—this two-thousand-year-old misunderstanding, Jarry explains, arises from the fact that biblical-era bicycles looked a lot like crosses. Oh, and Jesus rode his bike “lying flat on his back in order to reduce his air resistance.” Perhaps we should not be surprised that the Son of God was a prodigious cyclist; more surprising is the fact that he fell. And so perhaps the fate of the little girl I saw in the park is inevitably also our own. But when it’s our time to tip over, let it be at the last hairpin turn in the Golgotha racing track, and not stuck in some rut in McGolrick Park.
